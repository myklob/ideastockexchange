What this page does. It gives every belief about this topic one fixed address, so the thousand ways of saying the same thing collapse into a single entry and the real reasons to agree or disagree can be counted and weighed by evidence instead of by how often they get repeated. The address has three parts, one per table below: Direction (which way it runs), Magnitude (how absolute), and Specificity (where it sits on the general-to-specific tree). Same address means the same claim, so it merges; nearly the same address gets the redundancy discount. Full logic lives on One Page Per Topic.

💪 Continuum 2: Claim Magnitude, How Absolute the Claim Is (Modest ↔ Total)

How absolute the claim is, from a hedged "sometimes" to a flat "always," independent of which way it runs and of whether it is true. Two beliefs match on this axis when they are equally bold. See: Why We Need This Continuum.

Claim Magnitude Pro Belief at This Strength Anti Belief at This Strength Scope & Telltale Words
Modest (20%)
Hedged		 [Somewhat good, openly admits real flaws and exceptions] [Somewhat bad, concedes it works acceptably much of the time] Narrow. "Some," "tends to," "in certain cases."
Moderate (50%)
Standard		 [Clearly better than the alternatives in most cases] [Clearly worse than the alternatives in most cases] Definite but bounded. "Clearly," "generally." Where most serious arguments live.
Strong (80%)
Broad		 [Right across the board; the alternatives reliably fail] [Wrong across the board; fails wherever it is tried] Near-universal. "Across the board," "fundamentally."
Total (100%)
Maximal		 [Always right, everywhere, no legitimate exception] [Always wrong, has never once worked and never will] Total, zero limits. "Always," "never." One counterexample sinks it.

Key insight: The strongest arguments on either side usually live at Moderate (50%), not Total (100%). Attacking only the Total versions is a straw man. Engage the best version of the opposing argument, not the loudest.

🌳 Continuum 3: General to Specific, with Branching Subcategories (General ↔ Specific)

The same topic holds claims at every altitude, from a sweeping worldview down to one line-item policy. A general belief branches into subcategories, and each subcategory holds the specific beliefs beneath it. A belief only merges with another that shares its branch and its rung, so a worldview never gets double-counted with the policy it implies. A subcategory that outgrows a row graduates to its own child page (see Related Topics). See: General to Specific Framework.

⚡ The Engagement Landscape (Passive ↔ Active)

A stakeholder map, not a matching axis. It measures how far a person will go to act on a belief, which is a fact about the person, not the belief, so it never feeds the three-part address above. A casual supporter and someone willing to go to prison hold the same belief. See: Escalation Spectrum.

3. Principled Non-Compliance
Conscientious objector		 Refuses to act against conscience even at personal cost. Does not obstruct others. Refuses to implement or participate even when required. Does not obstruct others. [Thomas More pattern] [Official who resigns rather than implement]

Principled lawbreaking		 Openly breaks laws seen as unjust obstacles. Accepts consequences as moral leverage. Openly defies related laws or mandates. Accepts consequences as moral leverage. [MLK / Gandhi equivalent] [Objectors who accepted imprisonment]

Key insight: Engagement is independent of the three matching axes. Someone can hold a moderate claim (50%) about a cause they feel lukewarm toward (+40%) and still go to prison for it (Level 4).
