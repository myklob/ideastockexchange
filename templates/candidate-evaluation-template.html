🏛️ How Candidate Evaluation Works Inside the ISE

A user creates a belief page: “[Candidate Full Name] is the most qualified candidate for [Office] in [Jurisdiction].” It is filed at Elections → [Country] → [State] → [Office] and instantly inherits the same structural template every belief on the platform uses: Argument Trees, Evidence Scoring, Objective Criteria weighting, Cost-Benefit Analysis, and Conflict Resolution mapping.

No special configuration. No editorial setup. No custom rules for politicians. The architecture is identical whether evaluating a tax proposal, a scientific claim, or a Senate candidate.

The Criteria Come First — and Apply to Everyone

Before [Candidate Full Name]’s page existed, the [Office] node already contained a scored, community-built set of Objective Criteria: which qualifications matter most for this office, which policy domains carry the highest Importance Scores, and what measurable performance indicators distinguish effective officeholders from ineffective ones.

[Candidate Full Name]’s page inherits those criteria automatically. So does every other candidate. The goalposts are fixed before the campaign begins — and they are fixed equally.

Arguments Are Scored — Not Curated

When users submit arguments, each one is evaluated across three independent dimensions:

  • Truth Score: Is the claim factually accurate and supported by linked evidence?
  • Linkage Score: Does this argument actually connect to the question of [Office] qualification?
  • Importance Score: How central is this criterion within the pre-defined evaluation framework?

Outrage cannot override structure. Visibility does not equal weight.

No Agenda. No Moderator Bias. No Exceptions.

ReasonRank does not know it is evaluating a politician. It applies the same recursive logic used everywhere else in the system. The output is not “who dominated the debate stage.” It is “who scores highest under the criteria the public agreed upon before campaign noise began.”
