The Solution: Structured Reasoning

The Core Innovation: Organize Arguments Into Columns

+92 for fundamental breakthrough
Simple but revolutionary idea:
Allow everyone to "talk" at once if we allow users to organize their contributions. Give one page per issue, then let users post comments within a column of reasons to agree OR within the reasons to disagree column.

This simple innovation of allowing people to tag their post as a reason to agree or disagree will revolutionize the way we debate, come to conclusions, and think.

Visual comparison:

Post #2: "But what about X?"
Post #3: "I disagree!"
Post #4: "Off-topic comment"
Post #5: "Responding to #2..."
Post #6: "I support this too!"

Result: Chaos. Can't see arguments clearly.

‚úÖ ISE Format (Structured Columns)

‚úÖ Reasons to Agree

  2. Logic B
  3. Study C
‚ùå Reasons to Disagree
  1. Counter-evidence X
  2. Alternative Y
Result: Clarity. See both sides immediately.
What this enables (+90):
  • Compare quantity: Are there more reasons to agree or disagree?
  • Compare quality: Which side has stronger evidence?
  • See both sides: Can't ignore opposing arguments
  • Build cumulatively: Each person adds to existing structure
  • Algorithmic evaluation: Can count and score systematically

The Pro/Con Method: Tried and True

+88 for historical validation
Historical precedent:
  • Benjamin Franklin: Advocated making list of pros and cons for decisions
  • Thomas Jefferson: Used systematic pro/con analysis
  • Assumption (+92): Quantity and quality of reasons to agree/disagree has bearing on strength of conclusion
What we're adding (+90):
  • Internet-scale: Crowd-source brainstorming of reasons
  • Recursive evaluation: Pro/con list FOR each pro or con
  • Systematic: Database tracks relationships between arguments
  • Algorithmic: Calculate scores based on evidence quality
Example application:
FDR had to decide whether to join WWII. One pro: "German leaders were bad." But that pro itself has many reasons to support it. We can create a pro/con list for "German leaders were bad," then use that list's score to strengthen the original pro for joining WWII.

Not many people have time to do pro/con list for each pro or con. But on the internet, we keep making same arguments over and over. For thousands of years we've been repeating arguments Aristotle and Homer made. Most arguments have been made millions of times. No one has taken time to put them in database and outline how they relate to each other. We can change this.

Technical Implementation

Database Structure: Like a Family Tree

+85 for elegant technical analogy
SQL Project Description:

Basically it's a family tree database with these differences:

  • Parents ‚Üí Arguments (multiple arguments combine to support conclusion)
  • Children ‚Üí Conclusions (supported by parent arguments)
  • Gender tags ‚Üí Agreement tags (some tagged "agree," others "disagree")
  • Ancestor counting ‚Üí Scoring (count reasons that agree, subtract reasons that disagree)

Diagram: Arguments Support Conclusions

Explanation:

  • ‚ñ† Red box: Main conclusion
  • ‚ñ† Black boxes: Reasons to agree (supports conclusion)
  • ‚ñ† Blue box: Reason to disagree (opposes conclusion)
  • ‚ñ† Green box: Sub-reason (disagrees with one of the supporting arguments)

Net effect: Conclusion has 3 reasons to agree (in black) and 2 reasons to disagree (blue and green)

This recursive structure means arguments can support or oppose other arguments, creating tree of reasoning that extends until reaching verifiable data.

Database Implementation

Key tables:

  • Beliefs table: All conclusions and arguments
  • Relationships table: Links showing which arguments support/oppose which conclusions
  • Agreement type: Tag indicating whether relationship is "agree" or "disagree"
  • Evidence table: Books, articles, studies, data supporting each argument
  • Scores table: Calculated values based on supporting/opposing arguments

Algorithms for Ranking Ideas

The Basic Formula

+90 for systematic approach
Core principle:
How do you define a good conclusion? It's simple: A good conclusion has lots of good arguments that support it, and not very many good arguments that oppose it.

But how do you know if an argument is any good? Well of course the turtle stack goes all the way down: Good arguments have lots of good reasons to agree with them, and not very many good reasons to disagree with them.

Simplest formula:

Conclusion Score = (Reasons to Agree) ‚àí (Reasons to Disagree)

More sophisticated formula with recursive scoring:

Score = Œ£[(A(n,i)/n) √ó L] ‚àí Œ£[(D(n,j)/n) √ó L] / (Total Reasons)

Variable Meaning
n Number of "steps" current argument is removed from conclusion
- n=1: Direct support/opposition
- n=2: Reasons that support/oppose the direct reasons
- n=3: Third level of reasoning
A(n,i)/n Reasons to agree, weighted by distance from conclusion
- A(1,1), A(1,2), A(1,3): First-level supporting arguments
- Divided by n: Each level contributes proportionally less
- Reasons to agree with reasons to agree contribute ¬Ω point
- Third-level reasons contribute ‚Öì point
D(n,j)/n Reasons to disagree, weighted by distance
- Works same as A but subtracted from score
- More reasons to disagree = negative score
- j indicates each reason independent
Denominator Total number of reasons (agree + disagree)
- Normalizes equation
- Results in percentage (-100% to +100%)
- Or decimal (-1 to +1)
L Linkage Score (-1 to +1)
- How well does argument actually support conclusion?
- Prevents irrelevant arguments ("grass is green" ‚Üí "legalize drugs")
- Multiplied by argument score
- Weak linkage = less contribution to conclusion

Example: "It was good for us to join WWII"

Argument submitted: "Nazis were doing bad things"

  • Belief score: 99% (very high confidence this is true)
  • Linkage score: 90% (strong connection to WWII decision)
  • Contribution to conclusion: 0.99 √ó 0.90 √ó 0.5 = 0.445 points

Better argument submitted: "Nazis committing systematic genocide"

  • Belief score: 99% (historical fact)
  • Linkage score: 98% (genocide stronger reason for war than general "bad things")
  • Contribution: 0.99 √ó 0.98 √ó 0.5 = 0.485 points

Higher linkage score reflects that we don't go to war with every country that "does bad things," but genocide is stronger justification.

Quality vs. Quantity

+88 that both matter
The balance:
Allowing people to categorize their arguments as reason to agree or disagree will allow us to compare the NUMBER of reasons. But QUALITY of reasons is as important as number. There are many ways to evaluate quality of reason to agree or disagree with an idea.
Methods for evaluating quality:
  • User voting: Community rates each argument's strength
  • Evidence tiers: Peer-reviewed studies ranked higher than opinion pieces (see Evidence Tiers)
  • Recursive scoring: Allow users to provide reasons to agree/disagree with each reason
  • Linkage scores: How well does evidence actually connect to claim? (see Linkage Scores)
  • Expert weighting: Specialists in field have more voting power
Integration with statistical analysis (+85):
This discussion forum allows significant leap forward by integrating statistical analysis with debate. Confidence intervals can be assigned to each belief based on: - Number of reasons posted - Percent difference between reasons to agree vs. disagree - Amount of variance between evaluations - Quality distribution of evidence

Structure Reveals Complexity

+85 for forcing intellectual honesty
Why lists beat paragraphs:
Using lists that get to main point quickly do not allow for advertising. And that's what's being done when someone uses lots of words to describe their idea... they are advertising.

But when you use lists of reasons to agree or disagree, the issue becomes clear. You see the complexity. People don't want to see that there are good reasons to agree or disagree with both sides. They want to stick with their prejudices and previous conclusions. They want to gloss over everything with nice wordy paragraph that hides shortcomings of their thinking with wordiness.
What structured format forces (+88):
  • Can't hide weak arguments in prose
  • Must acknowledge opposing viewpoints
  • Complexity becomes visible

Web-Based Conflict Resolution

Applying "Getting to Yes" Principles Online

+88 for integrating conflict resolution theory
Core insight:
Everyone has conflict in their lives. Source of conflict is that everyone has different reasons for doing things. When we find ourselves in groups, what other people do affects us. Conflict arises when we do not understand or accept other people's reasons for doing things.

People's reasons come from their values and needs. These values and needs help people decide what should be done with problems they face.
Principle from "Getting to Yes" How ISE Implements It
1. Separate people from problem Vote on merits of idea, not person who proposed it
- Anonymous submission possible

- See underlying motivations (see Interests)
3. Generate variety of options Brainstorming section for solutions
- All possible solutions listed

4. Use objective criteria Evidence tiers provide standards
- Peer-reviewed data weighted higher

- See Objective Criteria Scores

- "What objective criteria resolve this conflict?"
