<p><span style="font-family: 'Segoe UI', 'Lucida Grande', Arial, sans-serif; font-size: 17px; font-weight: bold;">The Problem: The "Wall of Text" Trap</span></p>
<div>
<p>When we communicate in long-form prose without structural links, three major problems occur:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Hidden Foundations:</strong>&nbsp;Most arguments rely on&nbsp;<strong>Unstated Assumptions</strong>&nbsp;that the author takes for granted. Because they aren't labeled, the reader cannot challenge the root of the idea.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>The Pivot Maneuver:</strong>&nbsp;In a "wall of text," if you disprove one small point, the author can simply pivot to another part of the text. Because the "load-bearing" assumptions aren't identified, it is impossible to "topple" a false belief.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Cognitive Exhaustion:</strong>&nbsp;It takes immense mental energy to manually deconstruct someone else's argument. Most people simply "react" to the tone rather than "analyze" the logic.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr />
<h2>The Vision: Turning Prose into Architecture</h2>
<p>The&nbsp;<strong>Idea Stock Exchange</strong>&nbsp;is designed to treat beliefs like physical structures. If you were inspecting a building, you wouldn't just look at the paint (the prose); you would check the foundation and the load-bearing beams (the assumptions).</p>
<h3>Why We Must Link Arguments to Assumptions</h3>
<p>If we don't force this link, we are just shouting into the void. By creating a system that&nbsp;<strong>automates the decomposition of text</strong>, we achieve:</p>
<ol start="1">
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Logical Transparency:</strong>&nbsp;Every claim is explicitly tied to the assumptions required to make it true.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Modular Debate:</strong>&nbsp;Instead of arguing about a whole "belief," we can debate a single, specific assumption. If that assumption fails, the belief collapses automatically.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Auditability:</strong>&nbsp;We can generate a "Logic Score" for a belief based on how many of its underlying assumptions have been verified or debunked.</p>
</li>
</ol>

Most of this is fueled by the fact that we are arguing about the&nbsp;<em style="background: none repeat scroll 0% 0%; border: 0px none #1f1f1f; clear: none; display: inline; float: none; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px; visibility: visible; margin-top: 0px !important; line-height: 1.15 !important;">conclusions</em>&nbsp;of beliefs rather than the&nbsp;<em style="background: none repeat scroll 0% 0%; border: 0px none #1f1f1f; clear: none; display: inline; float: none; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px; visibility: visible; margin-top: 0px !important; line-height: 1.15 !important;">assumptions</em>&nbsp;that lead to them.</p>

<p>The process moves from a broad belief to a structured debate about its foundations:</p>
<ol start="1">
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Belief Submission:</strong>&nbsp;A user posts a belief (e.g., "Universal Basic Income will reduce crime").</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Taxonomy Placement:</strong>&nbsp;The belief is assigned a unique ID and nested within categories (Economics &gt; Social Welfare).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>Assumption Identification:</strong>&nbsp;Using standardized templates, users identify&nbsp;<strong>Stated Assumptions</strong>&nbsp;(explicitly mentioned) and&nbsp;<strong>Unstated Assumptions</strong>&nbsp;(logical prerequisites).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;"><strong>The "Necessity" Argument:</strong>&nbsp;This is the core engine. Users argue whether an assumption is&nbsp;<em>necessary</em>&nbsp;to accept the belief.</p>
</li>
</ol> 
<ul>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;">If Assumption A is false, does the Belief still hold?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;">If yes, the assumption is "Non-Essential."</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="padding-left: 4px;">If no, the assumption is "Load-Bearing."</p>
</li>
</ul>

<ul style="background: none repeat scroll 0% 0%; border-style: none; border-color: #1f1f1f; clear: none; float: none; margin-bottom: 16px; margin-left: 0px; padding-left: 27px; visibility: visible; font-family: 'Google Sans Text', sans-serif !important; line-height: 1.15 !important;">

<p style="background: none repeat scroll 0% 0%; border-style: none; border-color: #1f1f1f; clear: none; float: none; margin-bottom: 16px; visibility: visible; font-family: 'Google Sans Text', sans-serif !important; line-height: 1.15 !important;">The system must calculate a&nbsp;<strong style="background: none repeat scroll 0% 0%; border: 0px none #1f1f1f; clear: none; display: inline; float: none; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px; visibility: visible; margin-top: 0px !important; line-height: 1.15 !important;">Necessity Score</strong>. This isn't a "Like" button; it is a weight assigned to an assumption based on its logical impact. If the majority of users (or a weighted group of experts) argue an assumption is "Load-Bearing," that assumption becomes a high-priority target for verification.</p>
