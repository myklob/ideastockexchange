‚ùå Reasons to Disagree (and Rebuttals) Argument Score Linkage Score Weakening Amount ISE Rebuttal / Correction
Power Imbalance: "Assumes equal power; fails when one side has leverage." 40 50% -20.0 Incorrect. The book explicitly teaches BATNA for this exact reason. You don't need "equal power" to negotiate; you need a strong alternative. Improving your BATNA is how you deal with leverage.
Cultural Assumptions: "Reflects Western norms; doesn't translate to other cultures." 20 20% -4.0 False Distinction. Every culture faces the same binary choice: resolve conflict through reason (talking) or force (fighting). The requirement for objective criteria is universal, not "Western."
Good Faith Requirement: "Vulnerable to bad actors who fake cooperation." 50 40% -20.0 Nirvana Fallacy. We don't need perfection; we need a system better than the alternative. We can use AI to map the interests of bad-faith actors even without their cooperation.
Automation Complexity: "Too complex/emotional to be automated." 30 30% -9.0 Invalid Argument. "Hard" does not mean "Wrong." Complexity is why we must automate it. This is the only framework structured enough (Interests/Criteria) to be digitized.
Zero-Sum Games: "Objective criteria can't fix genuine scarcity." 40 40% -16.0 Logic Error. Even in zero-sum disputes, minimizing transaction costs (war/strikes) maximizes the remaining value. No other system handles zero-sum better.
Total Con Argument Points: -69.0

üéØ Integration with Idea Stock Exchange Framework

Algorithmic Foundation: The book's structured breakdown of negotiation processes provides templates for:

• Creating page designs that guide users through interest identification before position-taking
• Developing AI coaches that ask "What's your underlying concern?" rather than "What's your demand?"
• Building option generators that combine interests from both sides to invent new possibilities
• Establishing criteria databases of objective standards for evaluating options
• Implementing benefit-cost calculators that analyze proposed solutions for mutual gains

Scalability: With billions of people online, these automated processes could find willing conflict resolvers from each disputing group, potentially addressing conflicts at unprecedented scale. The book breaks down the process of the US settling disputes and uses that process to create algorithms, procedures, and simple page design templates that facilitate beneficial interactions between those who agree and disagree with a portion of a dispute.
